Tilt Switch

Tip the breadboard one way and the light turns on. Tip it back and it goes out. Inside that little metal tube is a tiny ball that rolls to bridge two contacts — so your Arduino can now feel which way is up. It's the same “read a switch and react” idea as a button, but the switch is gravity itself.

1 LED + 1 tilt switch 🔌 Pins 2 & 8 🔎 digitalRead + tilt ⏱ ~15 min

What you'll build

A tilt switch and an LED on a breadboard. When you tip the board so the ball rolls onto the contacts, the Arduino sees 5 volts on pin 2 and lights the LED on pin 8. Level it out and the light goes dark. The serial monitor narrates every flip so you can watch the code think.

💡

A tilt switch is just a button that gravity presses for you. The Arduino still asks one question thousands of times a second: “is pin 2 HIGH or LOW?”

What you'll learn

  • 1
    A switch can be anything

    Buttons, tilt tubes, magnets — any two contacts that open and close read the same way to the Arduino: HIGH or LOW.

  • 2
    digitalRead + the pull-down

    Read a pin as HIGH/LOW, and why a 10k Ω resistor stops a “floating” pin from twitching when the tube is open.

  • 3
    Mirror an input to an output

    One clean line, digitalWrite(ledPin, state), makes the LED copy the switch — no if needed.

Five things. The two resistors look different on purpose: the 220 Ω (red–red–brown–gold) protects the LED; the 10k Ω (brown–black–orange–gold) is the tilt switch's pull-down.

Before we wire

How a tilt switch works

A rolling ball inside a tube

The little metal tube hides a tiny ball and two contacts at one end. Tip the tube so the ball rolls down onto both contacts and it closes the circuit — just like holding a button down. Tip it back and the ball rolls away, opening it again.

Closed connects pin 2 to 5V → it reads HIGH. Open, and the pin must be tied to GND → it reads LOW. Gravity is doing the pressing for you.

Why the 10k Ω “pull-down”

If pin 2 connected only to the tilt switch, then when the tube is open the pin touches nothing — it “floats” and picks up electrical noise, flickering between HIGH and LOW on its own.

🎣

The 10k Ω resistor gently ties pin 2 to GND so a level (open) tube reads a firm LOW. Tilt it, and 5V wins through — the 10k just sips a trickle of current. That's a pull-down.

The main event

Wiring it up, step by step

The diagram is drawn the way it sits on your desk: Mega on the left with the USB & power facing away from you, the breadboard on the right with column 1 at the top-left. We build entirely on the a–e half (the side nearest the Mega). Use Prev / Next and watch the board light up exactly what to add.

Add now: the two power wires from the Mega to the breadboard rails.
5V (red) GND (black) signal resistor LED

Give the breadboard power and ground first — everything else hangs off these two rails.

  1. Red wire: Mega 5V → the breadboard's red (+) rail.
  2. Black wire: Mega GND → the blue (−) rail.
⚡ Both rails are live. The whole left edge is now power + ground.

The tilt switch is a small silver tube with two wire legs. Like a button, its two legs must sit in different columns.

  1. Push one leg into column 3 and the other into column 5 on the a–e half.
  2. It has no polarity — either leg can go in either column. Both ends are just two terminals.
Tip: stand the tube so it lies roughly flat. You'll tip the whole board to switch it, so leave a little room to move.
  1. Short wire from the red (+) rail to column 3, row a — the tilt switch's first leg.
Now 5V is waiting at one end of the tube, ready to jump across when the ball rolls onto the contacts.
  1. 10k Ω resistor (brown–black–orange–gold): from column 5, row b → the blue (−) rail.
This is the key part. It ties the switch's second leg (column 5) softly to GND, so a level tube reads a firm LOW instead of floating.
  1. Signal wire from column 5, row a → Mega pin 2.
Pin 2 now watches the switch's second leg: LOW through the 10k when level, HIGH from 5V when tilted.
  1. Signal wire from Mega pin 8 → column 8, row a.
  2. LED: long leg (+) in column 8; short leg (−) in column 9.
  3. 220 Ω resistor: from column 9, row b → the blue (−) rail.
🎉 Hardware done! Two wires to the Mega's pins, one tilt switch, two resistors, one LED.
✅

Two golden rules: a component's two legs always sit in different columns · the LED's long leg is + (toward pin 8), the short leg − goes through the 220 Ω to the (−) rail.

The brains

The code, line by line

It's already saved at sketches\15_Tilt_Switch. The idea is even simpler than the button: read the pin, then mirror it straight to the LED. Every line that prints is what you'll see scroll past in the serial monitor.

15_Tilt_Switch.ino

        

        

          
setup() — one listens, one drives
pinMode(tiltPin, INPUT) makes pin 2 a listener; pinMode(ledPin, OUTPUT) makes pin 8 a driver. Serial.begin(9600) opens the link the monitor reads.

          
digitalRead() — ask the question
int state = digitalRead(tiltPin); looks at pin 2 right now and hands back HIGH (tilted, 5V) or LOW (level, 0V). That one word is the whole input.

          
Mirror it to the LED
digitalWrite(ledPin, state); copies the switch straight to the light — no if needed. HIGH lights it, LOW darkens it, in a single tidy line.

          
Print only on change
if (state != lastState) means we narrate a flip once, not thousands of times a second — so the serial monitor stays readable. delay(20) is a tiny debounce so one tilt is one event.

In the Claude chat you can run either line by prefixing it with ! . The monitor runs at 9600 baud — press Ctrl+C to stop it.

When something's off

      
Troubleshooting

      
Almost every “it's broken!” is one of these — and the serial monitor usually tells you which.

      

        
💡 The LED is on all the time+
Pin 2 is reading HIGH even when the tube is level — the monitor will show tilted -> LED ON with the board flat. Usually the 10k pull-down is missing or in the wrong column, so the pin floats high.
Fix: Make sure the 10k goes from the switch's second leg (column 5) to the blue (−) rail, and that the GND wire reaches that rail.

        
🔌 Tilting does nothing+
The monitor stays on level  -> LED OFF. Either 5V isn't reaching the switch's first leg, or the signal wire isn't on pin 2.
Fix: Check the red wire from (+) rail to column 3, and the signal wire from column 5 to pin 2. Confirm each leg of the tube sits in a different column (col 3 and col 5).

        
🎲 It flickers or needs a sharp tip+
The light blinks near the tipping point, or only switches with a hard jolt. That's the rolling ball settling — totally normal for this part.
Fix: Tip the board a bit further past level so the ball commits to one end. The delay(20) already absorbs most of the jitter; you can raise it to delay(50) for an even calmer read.

        
🧪 The monitor is blank or gibberish+
No text, or random symbols.
Fix: The sketch prints at 9600 baud, so run .\scripts\monitor.ps1 (it defaults to 9600). If you opened another monitor, close it — only one program can use the port.

      

    


    
    

      
Level up

      
Challenge & extend

      
Got it working? Try one of these — small code tweaks, big payoff. Re-upload with the same command after each change.

      

        
Easy
Invert it
Make the LED stay on when level and turn off when tilted — a “don't move me!” light. Flip the write:
digitalWrite(ledPin, !state);  // was: state
The ! means “the opposite of”, so HIGH becomes LOW and vice-versa.

        
A step further
Count the tips
Add a counter that goes up every time the board is tilted, and print the running total — a simple motion tally:
// when a new tilt is first seen:
tilts = tilts + 1;
Serial.println(tilts);
Hint: only count when state goes from LOW to HIGH — you already track lastState.
