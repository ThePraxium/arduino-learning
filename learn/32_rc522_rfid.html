RC522 RFID card reader

Wave a card or keyfob over a little blue board and its secret number appears in the serial monitor. This is the exact trick behind hotel keys, work badges, and transit cards. You'll wire the RC522 reader to your Mega over SPI — a fast four-wire conversation between chips — and read each tag's unique UID.

RC522 reader 🔌 SPI on pins 50-53 ⚡ 3.3V power (not 5V!) ⏱ ~20 min

What you'll build

An RFID reader on your bench. Hold a card near the RC522's flat coil and the Arduino reads the card's UID — a unique string of hex numbers baked into the tag — and prints it. Tap a different tag, get a different number. The serial monitor shows you exactly which card just touched the reader.

⚠

One rule above all others: the RC522 runs on 3.3 volts. Wire its power pin to the Mega's 3V3, never 5V — 5V can cook the chip.

Before we wire

How a card with no battery talks back

The reader powers the card

The RC522 hums a 13.56 MHz radio field out of its flat copper coil. When a card drifts into that field, the card's own little coil harvests just enough energy from it to wake up — no battery needed. That's why a tag works forever and survives the wash.

Awake, the card whispers its UID back through the same field. The reader catches it and hands it to your Arduino.

SPI — four wires, one fast bus

The reader and the Mega chat over SPI. Picture it as a shared conversation: SCK is the metronome (clock), MOSI is the Mega talking, MISO is the reader replying, and SS is the Mega tapping the reader on the shoulder to say “listen up.”

🔌

On a Mega, SPI lives on fixed pins: SCK→52, MOSI→51, MISO→50. We pick SS→53 and RST→5 ourselves. Use those exact pins and the library just works.

The main event

Wiring it up, step by step

The diagram is drawn the way it sits on your desk: Mega on the left with the USB & power facing away from you, the breadboard on the right with column 1 at the top-left. The RC522 has seven pins in a row — we'll connect each one to the matching Mega pin. Use Prev / Next and watch the board light up exactly what to add.

Add now: place the RC522 reader on the breadboard.
3.3V (red) GND (black) SPI signal RST RC522

The RC522's 7-pin header plugs into one half of the breadboard so each pin gets its own column to wire from.

  1. Seat the module so its pin header drops into rows of the a–e half, around columns 1–7.
  2. From top to bottom the pins are SDA, SCK, MOSI, MISO, IRQ, GND, RST, 3.3V — we use all but IRQ.
No breadboard? The same wires go straight from the Mega's female jumpers onto the module's male header pins. The diagram still applies pin-for-pin.
  1. Red wire: Mega 3V3 → the reader's 3.3V pin (column 7).
Double-check this one. It goes to 3V3, the pin labelled 3.3 V on the Mega — not the 5V pin right next to it.
  1. Black wire: Mega GND → the reader's GND pin (column 6).
Ground is the shared zero that every signal is measured against. Without it, SPI is just noise.
  1. Wire: Mega pin 5 → the reader's RST pin (column 5).
RST lets the Arduino restart the reader cleanly when it powers up. The sketch sets this with RST_PIN 5.
  1. Wire: Mega pin 53 → the reader's SDA pin (column 1).
Confusingly labelled SDA, this is really SPI's SS (chip-select). On a Mega it pairs naturally with pin 53. The sketch calls it SS_PIN 53.
  1. Wire: Mega pin 52 → the reader's SCK pin (column 2).
SCK is the shared clock — the metronome both chips march to. Pin 52 is the Mega's hardware SPI clock.
  1. Wire: Mega pin 51 → the reader's MOSI pin (column 3).
Master Out Slave In — the line the Mega talks on. Pin 51 is the Mega's hardware MOSI.
  1. Wire: Mega pin 50 → the reader's MISO pin (column 4).
🎉 All seven wired! Power (3V3), ground, reset, and the four SPI lines. Time to upload.
✅

Two golden rules: the reader's power pin goes to 3V3, never 5V · the three SPI lines (SCK 52, MOSI 51, MISO 50) are fixed hardware pins on the Mega — use exactly those and the library handles the rest.

The brains

The code, line by line

It's already saved at sketches\32_RC522_RFID. The MFRC522 library does the radio heavy lifting; your loop just asks two questions — is a card here? and can I read it? — then prints the UID byte by byte.

32_RC522_RFID.ino

        

        

          
#include — borrow two libraries
SPI.h drives the four-wire bus; MFRC522.h knows how to speak to this exact reader chip. They turn pages of radio code into a few friendly calls.

          
setup() — wake everything up
SPI.begin() starts the bus, rfid.PCD_Init() boots the reader, and Serial.begin(9600) opens the monitor link. Then it prints RC522 ready - tap a card or fob.

          
The two gatekeeper questions
PICC_IsNewCardPresent() asks “is something on the reader?” and PICC_ReadCardSerial() asks “can I read its UID?” If either answers no, return bails and the loop tries again — thousands of times a second.

          
Printing the UID, one byte at a time
The for loop walks each byte of rfid.uid, prints a leading 0 for small values so every byte is two digits, and shows it in HEX. PICC_HaltA() then tells the card “we're done” so one tap is one read.

        

      

    


    
    

      
Send it to the board

      
Upload & open the monitor

      
The MFRC522 library is already installed. Plug the Mega in. From the project root in PowerShell, upload, then open the serial monitor to watch cards appear:

      

        
PS>.\scripts\upload.ps1 32_RC522_RFID

        
PS>.\scripts\monitor.ps1

        
In the Claude chat you can run either line by prefixing it with ! . The monitor runs at 9600 baud — press Ctrl+C to stop it.

        

          ✓ What success looks like
          
Upload ends with done uploading, the monitor greets you, and each tap prints that tag's UID:

When something's off

      
Troubleshooting

      
Almost every “it's broken!” is one of these — and the serial monitor usually tells you which.

      

        
🔌 The monitor prints the greeting but never sees a card+
You see RC522 ready - tap a card or fob. but tapping does nothing. Usually one of the SPI wires is on the wrong pin, or power is missing.
Fix: Re-check the four SPI lines against the diagram: SDA→53, SCK→52, MOSI→51, MISO→50, plus RST→5. Hold the card flat against the coil for a moment.

        
⚠ Nothing prints at all — not even the greeting+
The reader probably isn't powered (or got 5V and is unhappy). The PCD_Init() line runs once at startup, so no greeting means the sketch may be stalling on the dead reader.
Fix: Confirm power goes to 3V3 (not 5V) and GND reaches the reader's GND. Unplug, re-seat the power wires, and re-upload.

        
🧪 The monitor is blank or shows gibberish+
No text, or random symbols scrolling by.
Fix: The sketch prints at 9600 baud, so run .\scripts\monitor.ps1 (it defaults to 9600). If another monitor is open, close it — only one program can use the port.

        
📝 Compile error: MFRC522.h: No such file+
The upload fails before it starts because the library isn't found.
Fix: Install it with .\scripts\install-libs.ps1, then upload again. The library is what teaches the Arduino to speak RC522.

      

    


    
    

      
Level up

      
Challenge & extend

      
Got UIDs printing? Try one of these — small code tweaks, big payoff. Re-upload with the same command after each change.

      

        
Easy
Greet a known card
Save one card's UID and say hello when it taps. Print the bytes once, copy them, then compare:
// after reading the UID:
if (rfid.uid.uidByte[0] == 0xA1 &&
    rfid.uid.uidByte[1] == 0xB2) {
  Serial.println("Welcome back!");
}
Now your reader knows your card from any other.

        
A step further
Light an “access granted” LED
Add an LED on pin 8. Flash it green for an approved card, and you've built the core of a door lock:
// in setup():
pinMode(8, OUTPUT);

// when the UID matches:
digitalWrite(8, HIGH);
delay(800);
digitalWrite(8, LOW);
Swap the LED for a relay and it could open a real latch.
