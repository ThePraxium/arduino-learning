Project 4 · Beginner build

Build a working traffic light

Three LEDs, three pins, and a pinch of timing. You already know how to light one LED — now you'll command a whole intersection: green → yellow → red, looping forever. Take it one calm step at a time. You've got this.

Pin 9 · Green Pin 10 · Yellow Pin 11 · Red ⏱ ~20 min

What you'll build

A breadboard traffic light. The Arduino turns the green LED on for 5 seconds, switches to yellow for 2 seconds, then red for 5 seconds — and repeats automatically. Upload once and it just runs, narrating each light over the serial monitor so you can watch the code think.

💡

This is your first project that drives more than one output at once and sequences them in time. That's the whole idea behind animations, blinkers, and step-by-step machines.

What you'll learn

  • 1
    Driving multiple outputs

    Set up and control three separate digital pins instead of just one — the same blink you already know, repeated three times.

  • 2
    Sequencing with delay()

    Use timed pauses to make events happen in a deliberate order, one after another.

  • 3
    A shared ground rail

    Use a power rail as one common “ground bus” so all three LEDs share a single wire back to the Arduino.

Before we wire

Three lights, one shared ground

Remember the columns?

Each numbered column on the breadboard is one connected node: in our portrait layout the five holes a–e across a column are joined together (and f–j form a separate joined column on the far side).

That's why an LED's two legs must go in different columns — if both legs share a column they touch the same node and the LED is short-circuited.

New here: the (−) ground rail

Down the outer edges run the power rails — the red (+) line and the blue (−) line. Unlike a column, a rail is connected all the way along its length, making it a shared “bus.”

🎯

We'll use the blue (−) rail as one common ground. Every LED dumps into it through its resistor, and just one black wire connects that rail to the Arduino's GND. One ground, shared by all three.

The main event

Wiring it up, step by step

The diagram is drawn the way it sits on your desk: Mega on the left with the USB & power facing away from you, the breadboard on the right with column 1 at the top-left. We build down the a–e half (the side nearest the Mega). Use Prev / Next and watch the board light up exactly what to add.

Add now: the black GND wire from the Mega to the (−) rail.
GND (black) signal jumpers 220Ω resistor LED (+ long leg)

Before any LED, give “ground” a home. This single wire is what every light returns through.

  1. Take a black jumper. Plug one end into the Mega's GND pin.
  2. Plug the other end into the breadboard's blue (−) rail (the outer edge nearest the Mega).
🎉 The whole power-return path is set up. Every LED will borrow this one wire.

This is the one-LED blink you already know, just aimed at a named pin. Follow the current in order.

  1. Green jumper: Mega pin 9 → column 2, row a.
  2. Green LED: long leg (+) in column 2 (same column as that jumper), short leg (−) in column 3.
  3. 220Ω resistor: from column 3, row b → the blue (−) rail.
✅ One light down. The next two are exactly this pattern, shifted down the board.
  1. Yellow jumper: Mega pin 10 → column 5, row a.
  2. Yellow LED: long leg (+) in column 5 (same column as that jumper), short leg (−) in column 6.
  3. 220Ω resistor: from column 6, row b → the blue (−) rail.
You do not add another wire to GND — the resistor drops into the same rail you laid in Step 0. That's the whole point of a shared ground.
🟡 Two lights wired. You're cruising.
  1. Red jumper: Mega pin 11 → column 8, row a.
  2. Red LED: long leg (+) in column 8 (same column as that jumper), short leg (−) in column 9.
  3. 220Ω resistor: from column 9, row b → the blue (−) rail.
Final check — count them: three LEDs, three resistors (each running from a short-leg column into the rail), and ONE black wire from the rail to GND.
🎉 Hardware complete! Three lights, one shared ground. Time to give it a brain.
✅

Three golden rules: each LED's two legs go in different columns · the long leg is + (toward the pin jumper), the short leg − goes through the 220Ω to the rail · one 220Ω protects every LED.

The brains

The code, line by line

It's already saved at sketches\06_Traffic_Light. You don't have to type it — just understand it. Two ideas run the whole thing: drive a pin HIGH/LOW, then wait with delay(). Every line that prints is what you'll see scroll past in the serial monitor.

06_Traffic_Light.ino

        

        

          
Naming the pins
int greenPin = 9; (and 10, 11) gives each pin a friendly name. Now you write greenPin instead of remembering “9” — the code reads like a sentence, and if you rewire you change the number in one place.

          
setup() — three outputs
Runs once at power-on. Each pinMode(..., OUTPUT) tells the Arduino “this pin will push out voltage to drive an LED.” The new bit: you prepare three pins, not one. Serial.begin(9600) opens the link the monitor reads.

          
loop() — the sequence
loop() repeats forever. It turns green on, prints it, waits, turns it off — then yellow, then red, in order top to bottom. Reaching the end, it starts over, which is what makes the light cycle endlessly.

          
digitalWrite HIGH / LOW
HIGH sends voltage to that pin (LED on); LOW removes it (LED off). Turning one on, pausing, then off before touching the next means only one light is ever on at a time — just like a real intersection.

          
delay() makes the timing
delay(5000) pauses for 5000 milliseconds = 5 seconds. So green holds 5s, yellow delay(2000) = 2s, red 5s. Add it up: 5 + 2 + 5 = a 12-second cycle, forever.

        

      

    


    
    

      
Send it to the board

      
Upload & open the monitor

      
Plug the Mega in. From the project root in PowerShell, upload, then open the serial monitor to watch each light announce itself:

      

        
PS>.\scripts\upload.ps1 06_Traffic_Light

        
PS>.\scripts\monitor.ps1

        
In the Claude chat you can run either line by prefixing it with ! . The monitor runs at 9600 baud — press Ctrl+C to stop it. The light keeps cycling on its own even without the monitor open.

        

          ✓ What success looks like
          
Upload ends with done uploading, the green LED lights, and the monitor prints the cycle on repeat:

When something's off

      
Troubleshooting

      
Almost every “it's broken!” moment is one of these — and the serial monitor usually tells you which light should be on right now, so you can spot the odd one out.

      

        
🔌 One LED never lights (the others do)+
The monitor says GREEN - go but green stays dark. Most likely that LED is plugged in backwards — LEDs only let current flow one way.
Fix: The long leg (+) must be on the column with the pin jumper; the short leg (−) goes toward the resistor that drops to the (−) rail. Pull it, flip it 180°, re-seat it.


        
⚡ An LED doesn't light at all, even reversed+
Both LED legs may be in the same column. That shorts the two legs together, so no current flows through the LED.
Fix: Move one leg so the two legs sit in different columns (e.g. long leg in column 2, short leg in column 3). They must never share a column.


        
🌧 Nothing lights up at all+
The monitor scrolls happily through GREEN/YELLOW/RED but the board is dark. You probably forgot the ground wire — the single black jumper from Mega GND to the blue (−) rail. Without it, no LED has a path home.
Fix: Add Step 0: black jumper from Mega GND to the (−) rail. Also confirm each LED's resistor actually reaches into that same rail.


        
🤔 The wrong colour lights at the wrong time+
The monitor prints RED - stop but the green LED is the one glowing — a signal wire is on the wrong pin. The sketch expects green = 9, yellow = 10, red = 11.
Fix: Match the wires to the code: green jumper → pin 9, yellow → pin 10, red → pin 11. (Or change the pin numbers at the top of the sketch to match your wiring — just keep them consistent.)

      

    


    
    

      
Level up

      
Challenge & extend

      
Got it working? Brilliant. Try one of these — small code tweaks, big “I made it do that!” payoff. Re-upload with the same command after each change.

      

        
Easy
Speed up the whole cycle
Make the intersection feel like a busy city — halve every timing. Just change the three delay() numbers inside loop():
// was 5000 / 2000 / 5000
delay(2500);  // green
delay(1000);  // yellow
delay(2500);  // red
Smaller numbers = faster lights. Try your own pace!


        
A step further
Add a “flashing green” warning
Real crossings often blink green just before yellow. After green's 5 seconds, add a few quick blinks. Insert this right after the green section in loop():
// blink green 3 times
for (int i = 0; i < 3; i++) {
  digitalWrite(greenPin, HIGH);
  delay(200);
  digitalWrite(greenPin, LOW);
  delay(200);
}
A for loop repeats the blink — your first taste of looping inside the loop.
