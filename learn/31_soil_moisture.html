Soil Moisture Sensor

Two metal prongs in a pot of soil, and your Arduino can suddenly feel how thirsty a plant is. Wetter soil lets electricity flow more easily, so the reading climbs; dry soil resists, so it drops. With just three wires and one analogRead you've built a real plant-watering meter — the heart of a smart garden.

1 soil sensor 🔌 5V + GND + A0 🔬 analogRead 0–1023 ⏱ ~15 min

What you'll build

The fork-shaped soil sensor stuck into a little pot, wired to your Mega with just three jumper wires: power, ground, and one signal to A0. The Arduino reads the soil hundreds of times and prints a number plus a friendly label — dry, damp, or wet — so the serial monitor becomes a moisture gauge you can watch climb as you water.

💡

The whole trick is one line you already know from the photoresistor: analogRead(A0). It hands back a number from 0 to 1023 — here, how wet the dirt is.

What you'll learn

  • 1
    Soil = a variable resistor

    Water carries electricity. The wetter the soil between the prongs, the easier current flows — and the higher the reading climbs.

  • 2
    analogRead, 0–1023

    An analog pin doesn't just see on/off — it measures a whole range of voltage and reports it as a number.

  • 3
    Turning numbers into words

    A number is hard to read at a glance. We sort it into dry / damp / wet so a human knows instantly what to do.

Before we wire

Wet soil conducts, dry soil resists

The soil is the resistor

The sensor has two prongs. The Arduino quietly pushes a little voltage across them, and the soil between them carries the current. Pure water and the minerals in damp earth conduct electricity well; bone-dry dirt barely conducts at all.

So the prongs are really a resistor whose value depends on moisture. Wet → low resistance → current flows easily → a high number. Dry → high resistance → current struggles → a low number.

analogRead gives you the whole range

A button only ever reads HIGH or LOW — two values. An analog pin like A0 is different: it measures the voltage and reports a number from 0 (0 volts) to 1023 (5 volts).

🌿

We split that range into three friendly bands: below 300 = dry, 300–699 = damp, 700 and up = wet. Same idea as the photoresistor in Project 12 — just measuring dirt instead of light.

The main event

Wiring it up, step by step

The diagram is drawn the way it sits on your desk: Mega on the left with the USB & power facing away from you, the breadboard on the right with column 1 at the top-left. The soil sensor's little board rides along the a–e half (nearest the Mega). Only three wires — use Prev / Next and watch each one light up.

Add now: seat the soil sensor's three pins into the breadboard.
5V (red) GND (black) A0 signal sensor prongs

The small sensor board has three pins, usually labelled +, − and A0 (or S). Press them into three holes so each pin sits in its own row.

  1. Set the board so its pins land at about columns 4–6 on the a–e side.
  2. That gives + at column 4, − at column 5, A0 at column 6 — each on its own row of holes.
The two metal prongs hang off the end — those are what go into the soil. The little board just turns the soil's resistance into a voltage A0 can read.
  1. Red wire from Mega 5V → the sensor's + pin at column 4, row a.
The sensor needs a steady 5V to push its little test current across the soil. This is its power supply.
  1. Black wire from Mega GND → the sensor's − pin at column 5, row a.
Power needs a way back. Ground closes the loop so current can actually flow through the soil and home.
  1. Signal wire from the sensor's A0 pin at column 6, row a → the Mega's A0 analog pin.
🎉 Done! Just three wires: +→5V, −→GND, A0→A0. Now stick the prongs into some soil.
✅

Golden rule: match the labels exactly — + to 5V, − to GND, and the signal pin (A0 or S) to the Mega's A0. The A0 analog pins live on the row of header sockets labelled ANALOG IN.

The brains

The code, line by line

It's already saved at sketches\31_Soil_Moisture. If you've read the photoresistor, you've basically written this: read A0, sort the number, print it. The only new twist is turning the number into a word with one tidy line. Every Serial.print is what you'll see scroll past in the monitor.

31_Soil_Moisture.ino

        

        

          
setup() — just open the monitor
Analog pins need no pinMode — analogRead always works. So setup only calls Serial.begin(9600) and prints "Soil moisture sensor ready." so you know it's alive.

          
analogRead(A0) — feel the dirt
int m = analogRead(soilPin); measures the voltage on A0 and hands back a number from 0 to 1023. Low means dry soil; high means wet. That one line is the whole sensor.

          
The ternary — number into a word
(m < 300) ? "dry" : (m < 700) ? "damp" : "wet" reads as a question chain: “under 300? say dry. Else under 700? say damp. Otherwise wet.” A compact if/else.

          
Print & pause
The Serial.print calls build one line like moisture=620  (damp). delay(800) waits a beat between readings so the monitor stays calm and readable, not a blur.

When something's off

      
Troubleshooting

      
Almost every “the number's wrong!” is one of these — and the serial monitor tells you exactly what the sensor is reading.

      

        
🔢 The reading is stuck at 0 (or 1023)+
The serial monitor shows moisture=0  (dry) forever no matter what, or it's pinned at 1023. That means A0 isn't actually seeing the sensor.
Fix: Check the A0 signal wire — sensor's signal pin to the Mega's A0 (not D0). Then confirm + reaches 5V and − reaches GND; with no power the sensor can't read.

        
🍁 The number barely changes when I water it+
You pour water but the monitor's number hardly moves.
Fix: Push both prongs fully into the soil a couple of centimetres apart — if they're barely touching dirt, there's nothing to measure. Water the soil around the prongs, not the little board on top.

        
🔌 The reading jumps around wildly+
The monitor's number flickers up and down with no watering — damp, dry, wet, all in a second.
Fix: A loose pin. Reseat the three header pins firmly in the breadboard and make sure the jumper wires are snug. A wobbly 5V or GND makes the reading dance.

        
🧪 The monitor is blank or gibberish+
No text, or random symbols.
Fix: The sketch prints at 9600 baud, so run .\scripts\monitor.ps1 (it defaults to 9600). If you opened another monitor, close it — only one program can use the port.

      

    


    
    

      
Level up

      
Challenge & extend

      
Got readings streaming? Try one of these — small code tweaks, big payoff. Re-upload with the same command after each change.

      

        
Easy
Sound a thirst alarm
Add the buzzer from Project 9 and beep when the soil goes dry — a plant that calls for help:
if (m < 300) {              // too dry!
  tone(7, 880, 120);     // short beep on pin 7
}
Hint: wire a buzzer's + to pin 7 and − to GND, then add this inside loop() after the read.

        
A step further
A bar-graph in the monitor
Turn the number into a row of blocks so you can see the level grow as you water:
int bars = map(m, 0, 1023, 0, 20);
for (int i = 0; i < bars; i++)
  Serial.print("#");
Serial.println();
Hint: map squeezes 0–1023 down to 0–20 blocks. Add it right after you read m.
