LM35 Temperature Sensor

Three legs, no library, and your Arduino can feel the room. The LM35 is a tiny chip that turns temperature into a voltage — warmer means a higher voltage. Read that voltage on pin A0, do a little math, and the serial monitor becomes a live thermometer that updates once a second. Pinch the sensor between your fingers and watch the number climb.

1 LM35 sensor 🔌 Pin A0 (analog in) 🔬 analogRead + math ⏱ ~10 min

What you'll build

An LM35 temperature sensor plugged straight into the breadboard, with three short jumpers to 5V, A0, and GND. No resistors, no LED — just the sensor. The Arduino reads the voltage, converts it to degrees Celsius, and prints temp = 23.5 C every second so you can watch the temperature live.

🌡

The whole magic is one rule the LM35 obeys: 10 millivolts per degree. 0.23 volts means 23 °C. Once you know that, the math is just multiplication.

Before we wire

From warmth to a number

The sensor speaks in volts

A button is digital — just HIGH or LOW. Temperature isn't like that; it slides smoothly. So the LM35 gives an analog signal: a voltage that rises and falls with the heat. The rule is beautifully simple — 10 mV for every 1 °C.

At 20 °C the middle leg sits at 0.20 V. At 25 °C it's 0.25 V. Warm it with your fingers and that voltage drifts upward in real time.

How the Arduino reads it

The Mega's analogRead(A0) measures any voltage from 0–5 V and hands back a whole number from 0 to 1023. So 5 V maps to 1023, and each step is about 4.9 mV.

🧮

Two short multiplications turn that number into degrees: first raw × (5.0/1023.0) gives volts, then volts × 100 gives °C (because 10 mV = 0.01 V per degree, and 1 ÷ 0.01 = 100).

The main event

Wiring it up, step by step

The diagram is drawn the way it sits on your desk: Mega on the left with the USB & power facing away from you, the breadboard on the right with column 1 at the top-left. We build entirely on the a–e half (the side nearest the Mega). Use Prev / Next and watch the board light up exactly what to add.

Add now: the LM35 sensor, flat side facing you, legs in columns 3, 4 and 5.
5V (red) GND (black) A0 signal LM35 sensor

Hold the sensor so its flat face points toward you and the rounded back is away. The three legs point down into the board.

  1. Press the legs into three side-by-side columns: column 3, column 4, column 5 — all in row a.
  2. With the flat side toward you, the legs are, left → right: 5V (col 3), output (col 4), GND (col 5).
Orientation matters. If the flat side faces away, 5V and GND swap and the chip can get hot. Flat face = toward you.

The LM35 needs power to do its job. Its left leg is the + supply.

  1. Red wire: Mega 5V → column 3, row b (the left leg's column).
⚡ The sensor is now powered and already measuring the room.

The middle leg is the sensor's output — the voltage that means temperature.

  1. Signal wire: column 4, row b → Mega A0.
A0 is an analog input pin. This is the wire the Arduino actually "reads" with analogRead.

Every circuit needs a way back to ground. The right leg is the sensor's −.

  1. Black wire: Mega GND → column 5, row b (the right leg's column).
🎉 Hardware done! Three wires, one sensor — the tidiest build yet.
✅

The one golden rule here: get the LM35 the right way round — flat side toward you, legs left→right are 5V, output, GND. Three wires, three legs, three different columns.

The brains

The code, line by line

It's already saved at sketches\14_LM35_Temperature. Three short lines do all the work: read the pin, convert to volts, convert to degrees. Then print it. Every line that prints is what scrolls past in the serial monitor.

14_LM35_Temperature.ino

        

        

          
setup() — open the link
Serial.begin(9600) opens the serial line the monitor reads, then Serial.println("LM35 thermometer ready.") prints a one-time greeting so you know it's alive.

          
analogRead() — measure the voltage
int raw = analogRead(lm35Pin); looks at pin A0 and returns a whole number from 0 to 1023 standing for 0–5 V. That single number is the raw temperature reading.

          
The two conversions
raw * (5.0 / 1023.0) turns the count into volts; volts * 100.0 turns volts into °C (10 mV per degree). Using 5.0 not 5 keeps the decimals.

          
Print, then wait a second
Serial.print(tempC, 1) shows one decimal place, e.g. 23.5. delay(1000) pauses one second so you get a calm, readable stream instead of a blur.

When something's off

      
Troubleshooting

      
Almost every “it's broken!” is one of these — and the serial monitor usually tells you which.

      

        
🔥 The reading is huge (like 150 C) or the chip gets hot+
Almost always the LM35 is in backwards — 5V and GND are swapped. A reversed sensor heats up and reports nonsense.
Fix: Unplug, let it cool, and turn the sensor so the flat side faces you. Then left→right the legs are 5V, A0, GND.

        
🌡 The number never changes when I warm it+
The monitor prints a steady value that ignores your fingers. The middle (output) leg probably isn't reaching A0.
Fix: Check the signal wire from column 4 (middle leg) to A0. Pinch the sensor for a few seconds — a good reading rises a degree or two.

        
❌ It always reads 0.0 C+
A flat zero usually means the sensor has no power — the 5V or GND wire is missing or in the wrong column.
Fix: Confirm 5V goes to column 3 (left leg) and GND goes to column 5 (right leg), and that each leg sits in its own column.

        
🧪 The monitor is blank or gibberish+
No text, or random symbols scrolling by.
Fix: The sketch prints at 9600 baud, so run .\scripts\monitor.ps1 (it defaults to 9600). If you opened another monitor, close it — only one program can use the port.

      

    


    
    

      
Level up

      
Challenge & extend

      
Got it working? Try one of these — small code tweaks, big payoff. Re-upload with the same command after each change.

      

        
Easy
Show Fahrenheit too
Add one line of math and print both scales side by side:
// after computing tempC:
float tempF = tempC * 9.0 / 5.0 + 32.0;
Serial.print(" / ");
Serial.print(tempF, 1);
Serial.println(" F");
Now each line reads like temp = 23.5 C / 74.3 F.

        
A step further
Sound a "too hot" alarm
Reuse a buzzer or LED from an earlier project and trip it past a threshold:
// pick a limit, e.g. 30 C:
if (tempC > 30.0) {
  Serial.println("** TOO HOT! **");
}
Hint: warm the sensor with your fingers to test the trip without a heat gun.
