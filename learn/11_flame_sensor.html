Flame Sensor

Bring a flame close and a buzzer screams — a tiny fire alarm you built yourself. The flame sensor is an eye for fire: it watches for the infrared glow a flame gives off. This is your first project that reads a real-world danger on an analog pin and reacts the instant it sees it.

Flame sensor + buzzer 🔥 Pins A0 & 8 🔎 analogRead + if ⏱ ~15 min
🔥

What you'll build

A flame-sensor module and an active buzzer on a breadboard. The Arduino reads the sensor on A0 thousands of times a minute. When a flame comes near, the reading drops and the buzzer on pin 8 sounds the alarm. The serial monitor narrates safe vs. FLAME DETECTED so you can watch the code think.

💡

The whole trick is a number the Arduino measures over and over: “how much infrared light is hitting A0?” — and an if that sounds the alarm when it crosses a line.

What you'll learn

  • 1
    analogRead — a number, not just on/off

    An analog pin reports a whole 0–1023 scale, not just HIGH/LOW. Now the Arduino can sense how much, not only whether.

  • 2
    Threshold thinking

    Pick a cutoff number and compare to it. Above the line = safe; below = alarm. Tuning that number is the heart of every sensor.

  • 3
    Driving a buzzer

    An active buzzer is just like an LED to the code — HIGH makes sound, LOW is silence. Same digitalWrite you already know.

Parts at a glance

Just a handful. The flame sensor module is the little board with the clear/dark dome that points at the flame; the active buzzer is the round black can that makes its own tone the moment it gets power.

Before we wire

How a flame sensor "sees" fire

An eye tuned to infrared

A flame doesn't just glow with light you can see — it pours out infrared (IR), the same invisible warmth you feel from a campfire. The sensor's little dome is a photodiode tuned to exactly that IR. More flame nearby means more IR landing on it.

Here's the twist beginners trip on: more flame makes the number go DOWN. With no fire the analog reading sits high (around 512); bring a flame close and it drops toward 0.

Why a "threshold"

The sensor never gives a clean “fire / no fire” — it gives a sliding number. So we draw the line. We pick a threshold (this sketch uses 200): above it we call things safe, below it we call it a fire.

🔥

Read it like a rule: “if the reading drops below 200, sound the alarm.” Tuning that one number is how you make the alarm more or less touchy — the real skill of working with sensors.

The main event

Wiring it up, step by step

The diagram is drawn the way it sits on your desk: Mega on the left with the USB & power facing away from you, the breadboard on the right with column 1 at the top-left. We build entirely on the a–e half (the side nearest the Mega). Use Prev / Next and watch the board light up exactly what to add.

Add now: the two power wires from the Mega to the breadboard rails.
5V (red) GND (black) A0 signal pin 8 flame module buzzer

Give the breadboard power and ground first — the flame module and buzzer both feed off these two rails.

  1. Red wire: Mega 5V → the breadboard's red (+) rail.
  2. Black wire: Mega GND → the blue (−) rail.
⚡ Both rails are live. The whole left edge is now power + ground.

The flame sensor is a small board with three pins labelled AO, GND, and VCC (some say + / S / –). Stand it up on the breadboard with its dome pointing out, one pin per column.

  1. Push the module's three legs into columns 2, 3, 4 on the a–e half.
  2. From top to bottom the pins are AO (signal), GND, VCC — we'll wire each one next.
Tip: aim the little dark dome away from the Mega so you can wave a flame at it later without touching wires.
  1. Red wire: from the red (+) rail to the module's VCC pin at column 4, row a.
  2. Black wire: from the module's GND pin at column 3, row a to the blue (−) rail.
The sensor is now powered. Its little power LED may glow — that just means it's awake, not that it sees fire.
  1. Signal wire: from the module's AO pin at column 2, row a → Mega A0.
This is the key wire. A0 is an analog pin, so it reads the full 0–1023 scale of infrared the sensor reports — not just on/off.

The active buzzer has a + leg (often the longer one, or marked on top) and a plain − leg.

  1. Seat the buzzer with its legs in columns 8 & 9, + in column 8.
  2. Signal wire: Mega pin 8 → column 8, row a (the buzzer's +).
  3. Black wire: from column 9, row a (the buzzer's −) → the blue (−) rail.
🎉 Hardware done! Sensor on A0, buzzer on pin 8 — a complete fire alarm.
✅

Two golden rules: a component's legs always sit in different columns · match the sensor's labels exactly — VCC to 5V, GND to ground, AO to A0. Swapping VCC and GND can damage the module.

The brains

The code, line by line

It's already saved at sketches\13_Flame_Sensor. Two ideas run the whole thing: read the number with analogRead, then decide with if against the threshold. Every line that prints is what you'll see scroll past in the serial monitor.

13_Flame_Sensor.ino

        

        

          
Three settings up top
flamePin = A0 is where we read; buzzerPin = 8 is where we alarm; threshold = 200 is the line between safe and fire. Changing one number re-tunes the whole alarm.

          
setup() — wake the buzzer & monitor
pinMode(buzzerPin, OUTPUT) lets pin 8 drive the buzzer; Serial.begin(9600) opens the link the monitor reads; then it prints the “armed” greeting.

          
analogRead() — ask "how much fire?"
int flame = analogRead(flamePin); looks at A0 right now and hands back a number from 0 to 1023. Low means lots of infrared — a flame is close.

          
if (flame < threshold) — the decision
Below the line: buzzer HIGH and print FLAME DETECTED. Otherwise: buzzer LOW and print safe. delay(200) checks five times a second.

When something's off

      
Troubleshooting

      
Almost every “it's broken!” is one of these — and the serial monitor usually tells you which.

      

        
🔥 The buzzer screams all the time+
The monitor shows FLAME DETECTED! with no flame, and the analog number sits very low. Either bright room light / sunlight is fooling the IR sensor, or your threshold is set too high for your sensor.
Fix: Move away from windows and lamps, then read the analog= numbers the monitor prints when safe. Set threshold a couple hundred below that resting number.

        
🚫 A flame does nothing+
The monitor stays on safe even with a flame close. Usually the AO wire isn't on A0, or the sensor isn't powered.
Fix: Check the signal wire runs from the module's AO pin (column 2) to Mega A0, and that VCC→5V and GND→ground are solid. Keep the flame a safe few centimetres away.

        
🔈 No sound at all, ever+
The monitor may correctly print FLAME DETECTED! but you hear nothing — the buzzer is likely backwards or on the wrong column.
Fix: The active buzzer's + leg goes to column 8 (toward pin 8); the − leg goes to column 9 then to the (−) rail. The two legs must be in different columns.

        
🧪 The monitor is blank or gibberish+
No text, or random symbols.
Fix: The sketch prints at 9600 baud, so run .\scripts\monitor.ps1 (it defaults to 9600). If you opened another monitor, close it — only one program can use the port.

      

    

    
    

      
Level up

      
Challenge & extend

      
Got it working? Try one of these — small code tweaks, big payoff. Re-upload with the same command after each change.

      

        
Easy
Tune the touchiness
Make the alarm fire from farther away, or only when the flame is very close. One number does it:
int threshold = 350;   // was 200 (higher = more touchy)
Watch the analog= numbers in the monitor to pick a value that fits your room.

        
A step further
Add a warning light
Wire an LED (with a 220 Ω resistor) to another pin and flash it with the buzzer, so the alarm is seen as well as heard:
// in the fire branch:
digitalWrite(buzzerPin, HIGH);
digitalWrite(ledPin, HIGH);   // light too
Hint: add int ledPin = 7; and pinMode(ledPin, OUTPUT); like the buzzer.
