The RGB LED — every colour from three

One little dome that can glow any colour you like. Inside it hides three tiny LEDs — one red, one green, one blue. Dim and mix them with analogWrite() and you paint the whole rainbow from just three numbers. This project turns the PWM fading you already know into full-colour control.

3 colours in 1 🔌 Pins 9, 10 & 11 🎨 analogWrite × 3 ⏱ ~15 min

What you'll build

A single RGB LED on the breadboard, with each of its three colour legs wired through a 220 Ω resistor to a PWM pin (9, 10, 11). The sketch cycles through seven named colours — red, green, blue, yellow, cyan, magenta, white — and the serial monitor prints the exact R, G, B mix it's showing, so you can read the colour as numbers.

🌈

The big idea: a colour is just three brightness values. (255, 0, 0) is red; (255, 255, 0) mixes full red + full green into yellow. Light mixes differently than paint!

Before we wire

Three LEDs, one dome, every colour

What's inside the little dome

An RGB LED isn't magic — it's just three separate LEDs (red, green, blue) packed into one clear shell. Each colour has its own leg, plus they all share one common leg. In our kit that shared leg is the cathode (the − side), so it goes to GND.

The longest leg is that common ground. The other three control red, green and blue — each one driven by its own PWM pin through a 220 Ω resistor.

Why analogWrite gives you colour

You already used analogWrite(pin, 0–255) to fade a single LED. Here you do it three times at once — one value for red, one for green, one for blue. Those three numbers are the colour.

🎨

Light adds up, it doesn't muddy like paint: red + green = yellow, green + blue = cyan, and all three full = white. Same RGB numbers your screen uses for every pixel.

The main event

Wiring it up, step by step

The diagram is drawn the way it sits on your desk: Mega on the left with the USB & power facing away from you, the breadboard on the right with column 1 at the top-left. We build entirely on the a–e half (the side nearest the Mega). Use Prev / Next and watch the board light up exactly what to add.

Add now: the black GND wire from the Mega to the breadboard's (−) rail.
GND (black) red → pin 9 green → pin 10 blue → pin 11 220 Ω

This project only needs ground from the Mega — the three colour pins supply their own power. Set up the (−) rail first; everything hangs off it.

  1. Black wire: Mega GND → the breadboard's blue (−) rail.
⚡ The (−) rail is now ground — the RGB LED's common leg will tie here.

The RGB LED has four legs. Spread them so each sits in its own column on the a–e half, around row d.

  1. Push the legs into column 3, column 4, column 5 and column 6 at row d.
  2. The order on most kit LEDs is red, common, green, blue — the longest leg is the common ground.
Tip: gently fan the legs apart so each drops into a separate column. Four legs = four columns.
  1. 220 Ω resistor: from the red leg's column (column 3, row b) across to a free column.
  2. Signal wire: Mega pin 9 → the other end of that resistor.
Pin 9 is a PWM (~) pin, so analogWrite can fade the red channel 0–255. The 220 Ω keeps the red LED safe.
  1. 220 Ω resistor: from the green leg's column (column 5, row b).
  2. Signal wire: Mega pin 10 → the resistor's far end.
Same recipe as red, one column over. Pin 10 (~) now controls the green channel.
  1. 220 Ω resistor: from the blue leg's column (column 6, row b).
  2. Signal wire: Mega pin 11 → the resistor's far end.
Three colours, three matching resistors, three PWM pins. Pin 11 (~) drives blue.
  1. Short wire: the LED's common (longest) leg in column 4 → the blue (−) rail.
🎉 Hardware done! One RGB LED, three 220 Ω resistors, three signal wires, one ground.
✅

Two golden rules: each leg sits in its own column · the common (longest) leg is the only one going to the (−) rail — the other three each pass through a 220 Ω to pins 9, 10 and 11.

The brains

The code, line by line

It's already saved at sketches\11_RGB_LED. One small helper does all the work: showColor(r, g, b, name) writes the three pins and prints the mix. The loop() just calls it seven times with different numbers — one per colour.

11_RGB_LED.ino

        

        

          
showColor() — one helper, any colour
A function with parameters: hand it three brightness values and a name. analogWrite(rPin, r) sets red, then green, then blue — so one call paints a whole colour.

          
analogWrite — 0 to 255 per channel
Each pin is dimmed by PWM: 0 is off, 255 is full brightness. The three numbers together are the colour the dome shows.

          
setup() — three outputs + serial
pinMode(..., OUTPUT) for all three colour pins, then Serial.begin(9600) opens the link the monitor reads and prints "RGB LED ready - cycling colours."

          
loop() — the colour parade
Seven showColor() calls, each followed by delay(700), march red → green → blue → yellow → cyan → magenta → white, then repeat forever.

When something's off

      
Troubleshooting

      
Almost every “it's broken!” is one of these — and the serial monitor tells you what the board thinks it's showing, so you can compare to what you see.

      

        
🌈 Only one or two colours ever light+
The monitor prints all the right mixes (e.g. R:0 G:255 B:0 (green)) but a colour never appears on the dome. That channel's wire is loose or on the wrong pin.
Fix: Trace each colour leg through its 220 Ω to the right pin: red → 9, green → 10, blue → 11. Make sure each resistor and leg share a column.

        
⚫ The LED stays completely dark+
The monitor scrolls colours but nothing glows. The common leg probably isn't grounded, or the GND wire never reached the (−) rail.
Fix: Confirm the longest leg goes to the blue (−) rail, and the black wire runs from the Mega's GND to that same rail.

        
🔄 Colours look wrong (red shows as blue, etc.)+
It cycles, but “red” lights up blue and vice-versa. The colour legs are simply swapped — the kit's leg order can vary.
Fix: Either move the wires so each colour leg meets its named pin, or just swap the pin numbers at the top of the sketch: int rPin = 9, gPin = 10, bPin = 11;

        
🧪 The monitor is blank or gibberish+
No text, or random symbols.
Fix: The sketch prints at 9600 baud, so run .\scripts\monitor.ps1 (it defaults to 9600). If you opened another monitor, close it — only one program can use the port.

      

    


    
    

      
Level up

      
Challenge & extend

      
Got the rainbow cycling? Try one of these — small code tweaks, big payoff. Re-upload with the same command after each change.

      

        
Easy
Invent your own colour
Add one more line to loop() with any mix you like — this one is a warm orange:
showColor(255, 90, 0, "orange");
delay(700);
Try purple (160, 0, 255) or a soft pink. The numbers are yours to mix.

        
A step further
Smoothly fade the rainbow
Instead of jumping between colours, glide through them with a for loop that walks one channel up while another walks down:
// fade red down, green up
for (int v = 0; v <= 255; v++) {
  showColor(255 - v, v, 0, "fade");
  delay(8);
}
Hint: chain three of these to cross-fade red→green→blue→red forever.
